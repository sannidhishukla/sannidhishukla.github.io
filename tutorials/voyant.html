Finding texts

Voyant is an exciting tool for taking closer look at a number of texts all at once. The website gives you a number of options for how you'd like to pull up the texts you'd like to work with on the site from simply copying and pasting the body of text to finding text from URLs to uploading documents. The best way to get started with looking at the features of the sites, though, is with one of Voyant's premade corpuses.

In the bottom lefthand corner of the Add Texts box, click Open to choose one of the corpuses that Voyant has already prepared. Select your favourite in the dropdown menu and then click Open to start working. This option is helpful if you'd like to become familiar with the tools rather than work with a specific text.
Navigating Voyant

Now that you've added your texts, it's time to work with them. Voyant has a lot of cool built in tools but they're arranged rather confusingly. Thankfully, the tools page is broken into five distinct sections so it's easy to walk through them one by one.

Summary, Documents, Phrases
(bottom left-hand corner)

While it's a bit out of the way, the bottom left-hand tab is a great place to get started. Here there are three main tools.
  • Summary: The summary tool is handy for comparing the length and complexity of different documents. It will also tell you the most frequent words within a corpus and the words that are most unique to each separate document within it.
  • Documents: The documents tool is one of the less exciting tools but it's one of the most important. This tool will show you the length of each document in the corpus. Click Modify below to add, remove, or filter documents from your corpus.
  • Phrases: The phrases tool shows you where phrases are repeated throughout the corpus and the length of these phrases.
Contexts, Bubblelines, Correlations
(bottom right-hand corner)
Use these tools to get a better sense of how specific words are situated within the text.
  • Contexts: With this tool, you can input any word or group of words and see the phrases that come before and after them throughout the text.
  • Bubblelines: Input any word or group of words to see where they appear most frequently in each document. The document here is visualized as a line while bubbles of different sizes represent the words on the line depending on their frequencies at certain points in the text.
  • Correlations: For any word, here you can see how often its frequency varies with the variations in frequency of other words. You can also see how significant, in statistical terms, this correlation is.
Cirrus, Terms, Links
(top left-hand corner)

  • Cirrus: This tool gives you a handy word cloud that shows how frequent words are within the corpus or within a specific document. You can use the dropdown below to change what document you would like to work with and use the slider to change how many items appear in your word cloud.
  • Terms: Similar to Phrases, this tool shows you where words are repeated throughout the corpus. It also shows you which documents the words are most frequently used in.
  • Links: Enter a word or set of words and get a nice web-like visual that shows what other words tend to appear with these words. You can use the slider to change the number of words that appear.
Reader, TermsBerry
(top middle)

  • Reader: Read through your corpus. Click any word to see its use in phrases within the document in Phrases below and to see its frequency throughout the document in Trends to the right.
  • TermsBerry: This tool shows the most frequent words in a text. Hover over any word bubble and the other words that appear with it most frequently will light up too.
Trends, Document Terms
(top right-hand corner)

  • Trends: Enter any word or set of words to see how frequently they occur in the corpus or at any point within a document. Using the display option, you can change how this is visualized â€” whether as a line graph, as a bar graph, or something else entirely.
  • Document Terms: Similar to Trends, this tool will show you the relative frequency of each word you choose to examine across each document in a corpus. Here, though, your results are presented in a table rather than in a graph.
